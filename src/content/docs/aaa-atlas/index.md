Climate projections drive billions of dollars in adaptation spending. Whether you're authoring a Green Climate Fund concept note, contributing to a national NAP submission, designing a CGIAR research programme, or reviewing a proposal that cites climate model output — you'll be asked to defend the choices behind the numbers. *"Why these models, not others? Why this scenario? Why this baseline?"*

This section is the **CGIAR Climate Data Hub's reference answer to those questions**, written for the educated non-specialist. It exists because:

- Partner governments and proposal reviewers need to audit climate-data choices to trust the work that flows from them.
- CGIAR centres need to harmonise their methodology so partners see one consistent scientific voice rather than three contradictory ones.
- The choices behind a projection chart should be transparent and citable, not buried in code or in the heads of individual scientists.

## What this section will help you do

By the end of these pages you'll be able to:

- **Read a projection chart honestly** — central estimate, inter-model spread, scenario dependence, and what to do when models disagree with observations.
- **Choose defensible climate datasets and models** for an African region — and explain that choice in a methods section or to a sceptical reviewer.
- **Use baselines consistently** — and name the asymmetries that exist between observational and projection products so they don't look like errors.
- **Cite the science** behind your numbers with peer-reviewed anchors rather than vague references to "CMIP6."

## How to use these wikis

When you cite climate data in a proposal or report, link the relevant wiki here as your methodology reference. The pages are written to answer reviewer questions defensibly:

| Reviewer question | Page |
|---|---|
| *"Why this dataset, not another?"* | [Dataset landscape](/wikis/aaa-atlas/dataset-landscape/) → [Recommendations for CGIAR](/wikis/aaa-atlas/cgiar-recommendations/) |

## Audience and tone

The audience is the educated user who isn't necessarily a climate-science specialist — typically a climate-rationale author for a Green Climate Fund or Adaptation Fund concept note, a national NAP submission, or a research-for-development scenario brief. Jargon is defined on first use; the [Glossary](/wikis/aaa-atlas/glossary/) is the long-form reference for terms used across multiple pages.

Every page has a frontmatter version (`0.1-stub` → `0.2-draft` → `0.3-validated`) and a "last updated" timestamp. Pages marked *draft* are partial scaffolds; pages marked *validated* have been through a citation-verification pass.
